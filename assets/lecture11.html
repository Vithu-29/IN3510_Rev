1. Benefits & Disadvantages of Wireless LAN

Benefits of Wireless LAN

• Productivity, Convenience & Cost Advantages

  • Installation speed and simplicity.
  • Installation flexibility.
  • Reduced cost-of-ownership.
  • Mobility.
  • Scalability.

• Installation Speed & Simplicity

  • No cable to pull.
  • Eliminates current architecture obstacles.
  • Few transmitters/receivers for multiple users.

• Installation Flexibility

  • Network goes where wires cannot.
  • Not constrained by walls.
  • Easy to add more devices.

• Reduced Cost of Ownership

  • Mobile devices are cheaper than workstations.
  • Users can change location and stay connected.
  • No wiring closets needed.

• Mobility

  • Access to real-time information.
  • Supports productivity.
  • Enables flexible services.
  • Promotes user mobility.

• Scalability

  • Supports various topologies.
  • Easy configuration changes.
  • Works over large distances.
  • Useful for:
    • Small local networks.
    • Large roaming infrastructure networks.

Disadvantages of Wireless LAN

• Cost

  • Wireless network cards cost 4× more than wired cards.
  • Access Points are more expensive than hubs and cables.

• Signal Bleed Over

  • APs may pick up signals from nearby APs.
  • Overlapping signals may cause interference.

• Environmental Conditions

  • Susceptible to weather and solar activity.
  • Affected by buildings, trees, and terrain.

• Less Capacity

  • Lower bandwidth compared to wired networks.
  • Limit on how much data can be carried.
  • Packet loss affects performance.

3. Configurations & Hardware

Basic Hardware Requirements

To build a wireless workplace, you need: Access Points (AP), Adapter Cards (NICs), Directional Antennas, Extension Points, and Bridges.

1. Peer-to-Peer Network

  • Independent: Two PCs with wireless adapters connect directly.
  • Zero Config: Requires no administration or central server.
  • Limitation: Clients only access resources on the other specific computer.

2. Client & Access Point (AP)

  • Range Extension: Effectively doubles the communication range of PCs.
  • Infrastructure: Connects wireless clients to the main wired network.
  • Resources: Clients can access central servers and shared printers.

3. Multiple APs & Roaming

  • Use Case: Large facilities (campuses, warehouses) requiring wide coverage.
  • Roaming: Ability to move between AP coverage areas without losing connection.
  • Setup: Requires overlapping APs based on a site survey.

Standard Range Constraints:

  • Indoor: ~500 ft
  • Outdoor: ~1000 ft

4. Using Extension Points (EP)

  • Wireless Relay: Functions like an AP but is not tethered to the wired network.
  • Function: Relays signals from a client to a main AP or another EP.
  • Benefit: Extends network range without running cables.

5. Directional Antennas

  • Long Distance Bridge: Connects LANs in different buildings (e.g., 1 mile away).
  • Targeted: Antennas on each building target each other directly.
  • Setup: One antenna connects to the wired network; the other enables wireless in the remote building.

4. Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax)

Why Wi-Fi 6 Matters

  • Higher Data Rates: Up to 9.6 Gbps with 1024-QAM and 8x8:8SS support for 4K/8K and AR/VR applications.
  • Increased Capacity: 3-4x more throughput than 802.11ac via OFDMA and BSS coloring, ideal for dense deployments and IoT devices.
  • Reduced Latency & Reliability: Scheduled uplink/downlink OFDMA provides cellular-like QoS and deterministic performance.
  • Improved Power Efficiency: Target Wake Time (TWT) extends battery life up to 3x with optimized transmit/receive procedures.
  • Extended Range: Better outdoor coverage and high-efficiency operation in high-density environments.
4x Throughput vs 802.11ac

Wi-Fi Evolution:

  • Wi-Fi 1 (802.11, 1997)
  • Wi-Fi 2 (802.11a, 1999)
  • Wi-Fi 3 (802.11b, 1999)
  • Wi-Fi 4 (802.11n, 2009)
  • Wi-Fi 5 (802.11ac, 2013)
  • Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax, 2019) – High Efficiency Wireless (HEW)
