4. Transmission Hardware

Wireless communication systems consist of:

  • Transmitters
  • Antennas: Radiates electromagnetic energy into air
  • Receivers
  • In some cases, transmitters and receivers are on same device, called transceivers.

Transmitter Block Diagram Logic

Example: Creating a 900 MHz signal from a 300 MHz source.

  1. Source: Generates initial signal (e.g., 300 MHz).
  2. Amplifier 1: Strengthens initial signal.
  3. Oscillator: Creates carrier wave (e.g., 600 MHz).
  4. Mixer: Combines Source + Oscillator (300 + 600 = 900 MHz). Performs modulation.
  5. Filter: Selects the correct frequency.
  6. Amplifier 2: Strengthens final signal for the Antenna.

6. Evolution: 1G to 6G

0G

Pre-Cellular / 0th Generation Wireless (1940s–1960s)

  • Early Use: Mobile radio telephones used for military communication in the early 20th century.
  • Car-Based Mobile Radios (Mid-1940s):
    • Used a single large transmitter on a tall building.
    • Single channel used for both sending and receiving.
    • Required “push-to-talk” button — press to transmit, release to receive.
    • Used in taxis, police cars, CB radios.
  • IMTS (Improved Mobile Telephone System) – 1960s:
    • Introduced two-channel system (uplink + downlink).
    • Enabled full-duplex communication (no push-to-talk).
    • Had 23 channels operating in the 150–450 MHz range.
1G

First Generation (Early 1980s)

  • Technology: Analog signals (AMPS).
  • Speed: Up to 2.4 kbps.
  • Drawbacks: Poor voice quality, no security, poor battery, large phones ("Brick phones").
2G

Second Generation Wireless (Late 1980s – 1991)

  • Technology: Digital transmission; based on GSM, also includes GSM, IS-95 (CDMA), PACS (low-tier PCS).
  • Launch: First commercial launch in Finland in 1991.
  • Speed: Data rates up to 64 kbps.
  • Key Features:
    • Supports SMS, picture messaging, and MMS.
    • Provides better speech quality and higher network capacity than 1G.
    • Supports terminal mobility, personal mobility, and service profile management (PCS).
  • Drawbacks:
    • Requires strong digital signal; weak coverage leads to poor performance.
    • Cannot handle complex/high-bandwidth data such as video.
2.5G

GPRS Era

  • Overview: Bridge between 2G and 3G. Combined 2G with GPRS.
  • Speed: 64 - 144 kbps.
  • Features: Web browsing, Email, Camera phones.

Future: B5G (Beyond 5G) & 6G

  • Speed: 100 Gbps to 1 Tbps.
  • Latency: As low as 0.1 ms.
  • Reliability: Ultra-reliable communication.
  • Connectivity: Supports ~100 devices per cubic meter.
  • Coverage: Extended via satellite integration.
  • Security: Ultra-high security systems.
  • Power: Ultra-low power consumption.
  • Scalability: Highly scalable network architecture.
